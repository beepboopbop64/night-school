# The Similarity Meter: approved video script (Jake + judge, 2026-07-06)

The one thing this video says: comparing two complicated things becomes
possible when you write them both as lists of numbers, multiply the matching
entries, and add. That move (the dot product) is a meter for similarity,
with one honest flaw the next lesson fixes.

Vocabulary contract (one word, one meaning, all surfaces):
- The two qualities (entries): BASS and TEMPO.
- Overall size of an arrow: INTENSITY (intense vs subtle). Never "loud."
- Songs keep their widget names (Night Drive, Glass Rain, Slow Burn, Static).

Curriculum: lesson one. Plants: things are lists of numbers; multiply-and-add
compares them; raw scores can be gamed by size. Lesson two (attention) opens
on the unanswered walk-home question.

## Section 1: The app that knows you (0:00-0:35)
Voice: app plays a perfect song from an unknown band; a machine compared two
lists of numbers; tonight's question: how do you compare two lists and get
one honest score?
Screen: song card unfolds into bass 1, tempo 3; your taste beside it,
bass 2, tempo 1; empty scorebox with "?".
Why: motivation inside 30 seconds; question is the spine; no definitions.
Text link: page opens with the same scene; asks for a guess before scrolling.

## Section 2: Watch the obvious ideas fail (0:35-1:45)
Voice: try the obvious things with me. Idea one: add the song's numbers up;
(three songs' sums visible) the biggest numbers win no matter whose taste we
ask; an intensity contest, whoever is cranked highest wins. Idea two: count
exact agreements; (2 meets 1, red, no match) nothing ever agrees exactly,
near misses count for nothing. Idea three, the repair: let YOUR numbers
decide how much THEIR numbers count. Multiply matched entries, add.
(2x1 and 1x3 slide together, land on 5.) Big when you agree where it
matters. That is the whole recipe.
Why: recipe arrives as the fix for two natural failures (3b1b structure);
sums shown, not asserted; nothing false about what the viewer did.
Text link: page carries the identical worked example, then the widget.

## Section 3: Numbers become arrows (1:45-2:30)
Voice: two numbers make a point, a point makes an arrow: direction is the
mix (bass versus tempo), length is how intense it is overall. Watch the
recipe in this picture. (Taste list morphs into an arrow on the widget's
axes; a song arrow sweeps once; score ticks 5 to 0 to -5: together big,
right angles zero, opposite negative.) The recipe has a name: the dot
product, a.b (symbol lands NEXT to the visible 2x1 + 1x3 = 5).
The dot product is a similarity meter.
Why: arithmetic first, geometry second; the name is anchored to worked
digits; intensity = length planted here so Section 4 lands.
Text link: same arena as the widget; the page's next line is "now turn it
yourself."

## Section 4: The crack (2:30-3:15)
Voice: hold a song's direction, double its length. Every product has one
factor from that arrow, so every product doubles, so the sum doubles.
Exactly. (2x1 + 1x3 = 5 becomes 2x2 + 1x6 = 10, each product doubling on
screen.) So an intense, mediocre song can outscore a subtle, perfect one.
(The overtake, same rose flag moment as the widget.) The meter measures
agreement, but intensity rides along for free.
Why: the flaw sets up the next lesson; doubling demonstrated through the
products (the linearity intuition), no claims without pictures.
Text link: the widget's two challenges are this section made playable.

## Section 5: Take it with you (3:15-3:45)
Voice: the chain one more time: qualities became lists, lists became arrows,
multiply-and-add became a meter, the meter has a crack. One aside to prove
the recipe travels: score yourself against a job posting, same
multiply-and-add, one number for the fit. Now invent your own pair. The
question to carry home: what is the cheapest fix that keeps the direction
but forgets the size? Everything we just built is written out below this
video, so you can walk through it again at your own pace. And at the very
bottom of the page there are a few ways to keep going tonight, if you are
still up.
Why: recap + one marked aside + generate-your-own (Jake's consolidation
design); walk-home question stays open into lesson two; the page is framed
as a re-read tool, not a duplicate.

## AMENDMENT (Jake + judge, 2026-07-06): Section 2 attempts corrected

The failed attempts must be plausible moves (no strawmen), and the section
plays on ONE persistent stage: the taste column and three song columns never
leave the screen; every attempt happens on them.

- Attempt A: compare totals. Yours 3; Night Drive 4, Glass Rain 4, Static 17.
  Failure shown: (3,1) and (1,3) both total 4; totals lose the mix.
- Attempt B: measure differences entry by entry (Night Drive: 1 and 2, so
  3 apart). Reasonable, keeps the mix. Failure: it treats every quality as
  equally cared about; an indifferent 0 still gets punished. Distance is not
  what-matters-to-you.
- The repair: your numbers are VOTES on how much each quality counts.
  Multiply your vote by their amount per quality, add the votes:
  2x1 + 1x3 = 5. Why multiply finally has an answer: indifference (0) makes
  a quality count for nothing, passion makes it count double.
- Intensity cheating is NOT attempt A's failure; it returns in Section 4 as
  the dot product's own crack (Static's votes: 2x9 + 1x8 = 26).
