## So the meter isn't perfect. What's it missing?

Rebuild it in your head for a second. Two arrows go in, one number comes out. Multiply the matching pieces, then add those products up. That's the whole meter. Simple enough to rebuild from memory, which is usually a good sign.

Except simple isn't the same as complete. Take a candidate that already points the exact same direction as our target, and just stretch it, make every piece bigger without changing where it points. The score climbs. Nothing about the direction changed, but the meter reads higher anyway.

That's a real problem, not a technicality. A long, sprawling answer can out-score a short, precise one for no reason other than its size. A long song would beat a short one on sheer loudness, even if the short one matches the target's shape better. The meter is currently measuring "how much" tangled up with "which way," and it can't tell you which part of the reading is doing the work.

> What's the cheapest fix that keeps the direction the score is measuring but forgets how big the arrows were to begin with?

Sit with that one. We're not answering it here: it's the question that opens the next session. For now, just notice the gap. The meter we've got tells you direction and size at once, and that mixture is exactly what we'll learn to pull apart.
